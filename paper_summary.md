# Paper Summary

This paper introduces Tool-Lab, a framework for studying how large language models acquire information and make decisions under explicit resource constraints. The manuscript is currently being prepared in the Transactions on Machine Learning Research (TMLR) format, so the work is framed for a machine learning audience interested in evaluation methodology, interpretability, and decision behavior rather than only benchmark accuracy. Its central motivation is that most LLM evaluations focus on final answers while leaving the decision process opaque, even in domains where the way evidence is searched, compared, and prioritized matters as much as the final choice. Tool-Lab addresses that gap by adapting the Mouselab process-tracing tradition from cognitive psychology and behavioral economics to tool-using models, treating tool calls as observable search actions and measuring search depth, comparability, sequence, expenditure, and decision quality.

The paper positions Tool-Lab as a cross-domain methodology spanning political decision-making, consumer choice, and finance. Instead of giving a model unrestricted access to all information, the environment reveals evidence through controlled inspections, charges a monetary budget for reasoning and tool use, and logs the full action trace before the model commits to a choice. This makes the project both an evaluation framework for LLM decision behavior and a methodological bridge between human process-tracing research and modern AI systems, with the broader goal of understanding whether models search systematically, rely on heuristics, and respond sensibly to constrained information environments. At its current stage, the draft has a well-defined title, methodology, and detailed political-science experiment, while several other sections remain in outline form, so this summary should be read as context for the paper's intended contribution and submission framing rather than as a statement of finalized empirical results.
